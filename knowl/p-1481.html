Recall that a projection onto a vector is found by writing the original vector, \(\vec{u}=\vec{u}_{||} + \vec{u}_{\perp}\text{,}\) where \(\vec{u}_{||}\) is in \(V\) or
\begin{equation} \vec{u} = \vec{u}_{\perp} + \sum_{i=1}^n c_i \vec{v}_i\tag{3.5.3} \end{equation}
Finding the projection is analogous to finding the constants \(c_i\text{.}\) Take the inner product of ((3.5.3)) with \(v_k\text{,}\) the \(k\)th basis vector of \(V\) or
\begin{align*} \langle \vec{u}, \vec{v}_k \rangle \amp= \biggl\langle \vec{u}_{\perp} + \sum_{i=1}^n c_i \vec{v}_i , \vec{v}_k \biggr\rangle \\ \amp\amp\text{using properties of inner products}\\ \amp = \langle \vec{u}_{\perp}, \vec{v}_k \rangle + \sum_{i=1}^n c_i \langle \vec{v}_i, \vec{v}_k \rangle \end{align*}
since \(\vec{u}_{\perp}\) is chosen to be orthogonal to the vector space and since the basis of \(V\) is orthogonal,
\begin{equation*} \langle \vec{u}, \vec{v} \rangle = c_k \langle v_k, v_k \rangle \end{equation*}
Therefore
\begin{equation*} c_k = \frac{\langle \vec{u}, \vec{v}_k \rangle}{\langle \vec{v}_k, \vec{v}_k \rangle} \end{equation*}
