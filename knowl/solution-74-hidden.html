We have seen above that \(\mathcal{P}_n\) is a vector space and limiting this to the interval \([0,1]\) does not change that fact. We need to show that the inner product defined as an integral satisfies the three properties of inner products.
  1. \begin{equation*} \langle p, q \rangle = \int_0^1 pq \, dx = \int_0^1 q p \, dx = \langle q, p \rangle. \end{equation*}
  2. Let \(r\) also be in \(\mathcal{P}_n\) and \(a,b \in \mathbb{R}\text{.}\)
    \begin{align*} \langle ap+bq,r \rangle \amp = \int_0^1 (ap+bq)r \, dx = \int_0^1 (apr+bqr) \,dx \\ \amp = a \int_0^1 pr \, dx + b \int_0^1 qr \, dx = a \langle p,r \rangle + b \langle q,r \rangle \end{align*}
  3. \begin{equation*} \langle p, p \rangle = \int_0^1 p^2 \, dx \end{equation*}
    and this is greater than or equal or zero and can be shown that equals 0 if and only if \(p(x)\equiv 0\text{.}\)
