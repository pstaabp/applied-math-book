In light of projections, we can reframe this problem. We are seeking the point on \(L\) closest to \(P\text{.}\) This can be found by projecting the vector \(\overrightarrow{OP}\) to the line \(L\) or
\begin{equation*} \text{proj}_{\vec{v}} \overrightarrow{OP} = \frac{\langle \vec{v}, \overrightarrow{OP} \rangle} {\langle \vec{v},\vec{v}\rangle} \vec{v} \end{equation*}
and in this case, with \(\vec{v} = [2\;\;1]^{\intercal}\) and \(\overrightarrow{OP}=[1\;\;4]^{\intercal}\text{,}\)
\begin{equation*} \text{proj}_{\vec{v}} \overrightarrow{OP} = \frac{\langle \vec{v}, \overrightarrow{OP} \rangle} {\langle \vec{v},\vec{v}\rangle} = \frac{6}{5} \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}= \begin{bmatrix} 12/5 \\ 6/5 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
which is the same result as from Calculus.
