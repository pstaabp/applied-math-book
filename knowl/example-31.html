Example 1.5.12.

Is the matrix
\begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix} 3 \amp 2 \\ 1 \amp 4 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
singular or nonsingular?
Solution.
We can consider the elements of the matrix to be the coefficients of a homogeneous linear system and find its solution. Thus we reduce
\begin{align*} \left[\begin{array}{rr|r} 3 \amp 2 \amp 0 \\ 1 \amp 4 \amp 0 \end{array}\right] \\ -R_1 + 3R_2 \rightarrow R_2 \qquad \left[\begin{array}{rr|r} 3 \amp 2 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 10 \amp 0 \end{array}\right] \end{align*}
which is now in echelon form. The last equation \(10y=0\) implies \(y=0\) and the top row can be written as \(3x+2y=0\) or \(3x=0\) or \(x=0\text{,}\) thus this has the unique solution \((0,0)\text{.}\) This implies that the original matrix is nonsingular.
